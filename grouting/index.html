Grouting Design for Kelowna’s Variable Glaciofluvial Ground

The pump packer assembly sits on the deck of the rig, with a 4-inch circulation line snaking down into the overburden. In Kelowna, we run double-packer setups more often than not because the glaciofluvial deposits change every few meters. A split-spoon sample from 4.5 m depth might show clean sand, then at 6 m you hit a dense silt till full of rounded cobbles. That layered profile is what makes grouting design here a real engineering exercise. You cannot just take a generic mix design off the shelf and expect it to penetrate the matrix around Knox Mountain or stabilize an excavation bench near the lake shore. The single most important input for a grouting program in this city is a careful log of the subsurface variability, because permeability can swing by two orders of magnitude across a single block. We often cross-check the grout take predictions with in-situ permeability data from falling-head tests in the same boreholes, and when there is uncertainty about the fines content we run a grain-size curve to adjust the w/c ratio and the bentonite percentage.

A well-designed grouting program in Kelowna hinges on the grout-soil interaction, not just the cement chemistry.

Methodology and scope

One thing we see repeatedly in Kelowna is that the contact zone between the till and the underlying bedrock is rarely a clean interface. More often, there is a 0.5 to 2 m transition of weathered rock fragments suspended in a silty matrix. If you treat that zone with a neat cement grout at high pressure, you risk hydro-fracturing the till and losing circulation into the lake or into nearby utility trenches. Our grouting design starts with a rheological characterization of the grout: Marsh funnel viscosity, bleed under pressure, and gel time measured in the lab at the temperature of the groundwater. For jobs downtown, where the water table sits high, we specify microfine cements with a Blaine fineness above 800 m²/kg and a pressure limit of 1.5 bar per meter of overburden. In the Mission neighborhood, where the ground is drier but contains more cobbles, we switch to a coarser Type GU blend with a higher sand load to reduce shrinkage. Every design is backed by a groutability ratio calculated from the D15 of the soil and the D85 of the grout, following the criteria of Mitchell and Kauschinger. For permanent cut-off curtains, we require a pre-construction test panel, instrumented with piezometers, to validate that the achieved residual permeability stays below 5x10⁻⁶ cm/s.

Local considerations

The soil conditions between the Pandosy corridor and the Glenmore bench illustrate the risk spread. Pandosy sits on loose-to-medium fluvial sands with high groundwater that communicates directly with Okanagan Lake; a grouting program there has to prevent erosion of fines and deal with artesian conditions in spring. Glenmore, by contrast, rests on a thick clay-silt till with low permeability but high shrink-swell potential. If you inject a high-bleed grout into that till, the bleed water can saturate the clay lenses and trigger differential heave before the cement sets. The worst scenario we have investigated was a retaining wall that tilted 3 degrees because post-grouting pore pressures were not allowed to dissipate. That is why the grouting design must specify a verification program: water pressure monitoring during injection, refusal criteria based on volume and pressure, and post-grouting coring to check the continuity of the bulb or curtain. Skipping the monitoring phase in Kelowna is a gamble that rarely pays off.

Associated technical services

01

Permeation Grouting Design

For loose sandy layers beneath buildings in the downtown core, we design sodium-silicate or microfine-cement permeation programs that fill the pore space without disturbing the soil structure. Includes groutability assessment and on-site gel time adjustment.

02

Compaction Grouting in Till

Where the Glenmore till is too coarse for permeation, we specify low-mobility grout injected at controlled pressure to densify the matrix. The design sets refusal criteria and volume limits per stage to limit heave.

03

Test Panel and QA/QC Program

Before production grouting, we lay out a test panel with primary, secondary, and tertiary holes, instrumented with vibrating-wire piezometers. The data feeds back into the final design to confirm spacing and injection pressure.

Frequently asked questions

What type of grout works best in Kelowna’s silty till?

For the dense silty till common in neighborhoods like Glenmore and the Upper Mission, compaction grouting with a low-slump, low-bleed mortar is often more effective than permeation grouting. The mix typically uses a Type GU cement with a sand-to-cement ratio around 2.5:1 and a slump below 50 mm, injected in stages to densify the matrix without hydro-fracturing.

How long does a grouting design and test panel take?

A complete package, from the first borehole to the approved production parameters, usually spans three to five weeks. The test panel alone requires about ten days: two days for drilling and grouting, and eight days for pressure monitoring and coring to confirm the grout has set and the permeability target has been met.

What's the typical cost range for a grouting design in Kelowna?

For a residential or light commercial project, the design and supervision fee falls between CA$1,630 and CA$5,180, depending on the number of grout stages, the depth of treatment, and the instrumentation required for the test panel. Larger infrastructure jobs are quoted after a scoping review.

Do you use microfine cements for all groundwater cut-off walls?

We specify microfine cement when the target soil has a D15 below 0.3 mm and the required residual permeability is under 1×10⁻⁵ cm/s. In Kelowna, this is most common near the lakeshore, where the sand is fine and the groundwater gradient is steep. For coarser deposits, a well-graded Type GU blend with bentonite can achieve the same seal at a lower cost.

How do you prevent grout from migrating into the lake?

Grout migration is controlled by setting a strict injection pressure limit, usually 1 bar per meter of overburden, and by using a quick-setting grout with a gel time under 60 seconds for the outer row of the curtain. We monitor water turbidity and piezometric pressure in real time during the injection, and we stop the pump the moment the pressure-volume curve deviates from the design envelope.
