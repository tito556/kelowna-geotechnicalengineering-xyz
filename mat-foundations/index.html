Raft/Mat Foundation Design for Kelowna's Glacial Lakebed Soils

Together, we solve the challenges of tomorrow.

DETAILS →

Kelowna sits at 344 m elevation on the bed of a former glacial lake, and the Okanagan Valley's thick sequence of lacustrine silts and clays demands a foundation system that bridges soft zones. Raft/mat foundation design becomes the practical choice when strip footings would spread too wide and differential settlement threatens the superstructure. The city's 2021 census count of 144,576 people continues climbing, pushing construction onto marginal land near Mill Creek and the Mission, where subsurface variability is high. We supply the lab-measured modulus of subgrade reaction, consolidation parameters, and undrained shear strength that structural engineers need for a compliant raft design under NBCC 2020 and CSA A23.3. The same silty matrix that holds up orchards and vineyards creates a bearing challenge—our triaxial testing quantifies the stress-strain response directly from Shelby tube samples extracted across the footprint.

A raft foundation in Kelowna works because it floats over soft spots. The lab data tells you how far it will settle and how fast.

Method and coverage

A common mistake on Okanagan projects is ordering a raft foundation but skipping the consolidation test, then dealing with long-term settlement complaints two years after occupancy. Raft/mat foundation design relies on accurate compressibility data—Cc, Cr, and cv—to predict settlement under the mat's full contact pressure. We run incremental oedometer tests per CSA + ASTM D2435 on undisturbed specimens from each distinct soil layer. For siltier profiles near the lake, we also check the collapsible potential because some of Kelowna's upper silts are meta-stable when wetted. The raft thickness and reinforcement layout follow from the pressure distribution we help define. When granular pads are needed to replace unsuitable near-surface material, we confirm compaction through proctor-tests and sand cone density checks, so the subgrade beneath the mat meets the specified stiffness before concrete is placed. Our lab reports deliver the full set of parameters: Cu, φ', k, and mv.

Regional considerations

On a Mission-area project we sampled a clay seam at 4 m depth that looked firm in the trench wall but returned a Su of just 18 kPa in the triaxial cell. That soft lens, if left undetected, would have concentrated stress under the raft edge and caused rotational settlement. Raft/mat foundation design without targeted sampling misses these thin, weak horizons. Kelowna's post-glacial stratigraphy is full of them—laminated silts with interbedded clay layers deposited as the ice retreated. We also measure the groundwater level at the time of drilling; spring snowmelt pushes the water table up against the underside of the mat in April and May, changing the buoyancy calculation. The NBCC 2020 requires a serviceability limit state check that depends on the soil stiffness profile we provide, and missing it means a cracked slab and an expensive repair.

Common questions

What soil parameters do you test for a raft/mat foundation design in Kelowna?

We test undrained shear strength via triaxial compression, consolidation characteristics (Cc, cv, OCR), effective friction angle, and collapse potential. We also measure in-situ moisture and density on every sample. The report provides the modulus of subgrade reaction and a settlement estimate for the given bearing pressure.

How much does a raft foundation geotechnical investigation cost in Kelowna?

A typical investigation with drilling, undisturbed sampling, and lab testing for raft/mat foundation design ranges from CA$1,240 to CA$6,530, depending on the number of boreholes, depth to competent material, and the suite of tests required. Sites near the lake with thicker soft zones usually need more sampling points.

How deep do you drill for a raft foundation in the Okanagan Valley?

Borehole depth follows the 2:1 stress distribution rule. We drill to at least twice the mat width below the bearing level, or until we reach a competent stratum with Su above 75 kPa. In Kelowna's valley center that often means 10 to 15 m depth.
